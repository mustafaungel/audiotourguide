Stop 1: General Introduction & Outside Orientation

*“Welcome to Kaymaklı, one of the most remarkable underground cities of
Cappadocia. Before we step inside, take a moment here at the entrance to
imagine what lies below your feet. Beneath this quiet village stretches
a vast labyrinth carved entirely by hand, layer after layer into the
volcanic rock.

Kaymaklı is the largest underground settlement open to visitors in
Cappadocia. Archaeologists believe its first chambers were carved during
the Hittite period, around 3,500 years ago. Later, in the early
Christian era, communities expanded it into a city beneath the earth. At
its peak, Kaymaklı could shelter thousands of people — some estimates
say as many as 3,500 at once — along with their animals, food, and wine.

Why live underground? The answer is survival. Cappadocia’s soft volcanic
stone made it possible to carve whole cities into the earth. When
invasions swept through Anatolia, from Arab raids in the 7th century to
Mongols in the 13th, local people could retreat underground for days,
even weeks, completely hidden.

Life here was organized and ingenious. The city has stables, kitchens,
living rooms, churches, storage cellars, wineries, and even a system of
ventilation shafts that still works today. Huge circular stone doors
could seal passages, turning the city into a fortress invisible from
above.

As you prepare to enter, remember: this is not a museum in the usual
sense. You are about to walk through the very tunnels and rooms where
generations of Cappadocians once lived, prayed, and survived. The walls
you see were scratched by hands centuries ago, and every chamber tells a
story of resilience.

Now, let’s begin our descent. Ahead of you is a narrow, sloping passage
— the first gateway into the underground city. Please mind your head as
you enter; the ceilings are low, because this city was built for
defense, not comfort. Step carefully, and let’s move together to the
first chamber.”*

Stop 2: Entrance Passage

*“Now, as you begin your descent into Kaymaklı, notice how the daylight
fades and the tunnel narrows around you. This first passage is your
threshold — leaving behind the open world above and entering the hidden
world beneath. The ceiling is low; you’ll need to bend slightly, just as
people did centuries ago. That was intentional. Narrow corridors made it
impossible for invaders to storm in with speed or weapons raised. Every
curve and slope was designed for defense, forcing outsiders to move
slowly, while locals knew the path by heart.

Look at the walls closely. Can you see the grooves and marks in the
stone? These are the fingerprints of history, carved by hand with simple
iron tools. Imagine villagers working by flickering torchlight, their
hammers echoing through the rock. Some carried baskets of rubble to the
surface, others steadied oil lamps to give light. Even this entrance
passage was the product of generations of labor.

As you walk, picture the families who passed through here. Mothers
carrying infants wrapped in blankets, children clutching baskets of
bread, fathers leading animals toward the stables ahead. During times of
invasion, these tunnels filled with the noise of hurried footsteps, the
lowing of cattle, and the anxious whispers of neighbors. The deeper they
went, the safer they felt.

But this passage was more than just a road downward — it was also a
barrier. Defenders could block sections with heavy stone doors, or
attack intruders from above through small openings. What feels to us
like a simple corridor was, in their eyes, part of a fortress.

Take a deep breath as you continue forward. You’re leaving the open air
behind and entering a world that was carefully planned to keep life
hidden and secure. Ahead lies the first major chamber of Kaymaklı: the
stables, where the underground story truly begins.”*

Stop 3: Stables (First Level)

*“You have now arrived in the stables — one of the most important spaces
of the underground city. This wide chamber was designed to house the
animals that families depended on for survival. Look carefully at the
long troughs carved directly into the stone along the walls. These are
feeding basins, worn smooth by centuries of use. Goats, sheep, and
sometimes even horses were tethered here, their bodies filling the space
with warmth and sound.

It may seem strange to keep animals underground, but for the people of
Kaymaklı it was essential. Their flocks provided milk, cheese, wool, and
meat — the staples of daily life. During times of invasion, livestock
meant survival. Without them, families could not endure weeks of hiding.
Imagine the scene: the bleating of goats echoing through the tunnels,
the musty smell of hay stacked in corners, the nervous hands of children
trying to comfort the animals while danger raged above.

The stables also served another purpose: warmth. In the cold Anatolian
winters, the body heat of animals helped raise the underground
temperature. Their presence turned these caves into a living, breathing
shelter. Archaeologists believe that the first level was chosen for
stables so that waste and smells would be easier to remove, while also
making it simple to lead animals in quickly from the surface.

Think about the daily routine. At dawn, families would feed and water
their animals, making sure each was secure. Children often helped with
these chores, learning from an early age how vital livestock were. In
times of peace, the stables may have been used occasionally, but during
attacks they became crowded, noisy sanctuaries of survival.

Take one more look at these troughs and the open space around you. This
was not just an animal pen — it was the beating heart of life
underground, ensuring that food, warmth, and security were preserved.
Now, follow the sloping corridor ahead, and you will reach the storage
rooms where provisions for both humans and animals were kept.”*

Stop 4 – Storage Rooms (First Level)

You are now inside the storage chambers of Kaymaklı’s first level. These
rooms were the heartbeat of long-term survival. Look at the carved
niches in the walls — each one once held large clay jars called
amphorae. Inside them, families stored grain, beans, dried fruit, olive
oil, and wine. The cool, stable temperature underground acted as a
natural refrigerator, keeping supplies fresh for weeks or even months.
Picture the effort it took to fill these storerooms. Before winter or in
times of threat, villagers worked together to stockpile food. Grain
would be carried in woven sacks, poured carefully into containers. Olive
oil was sealed with cloth and wax. Children may have helped by fetching
smaller jars or sweeping the floor.
During invasions, these rooms became more than pantries; they were
lifelines. Imagine families gathered here, measuring out rations
carefully, whispering prayers that the food would last until danger
passed. Each chamber tells us that life underground was not chaotic but
highly organized.
As you leave this chamber, follow the passage downward. Ahead lies the
second level, where living quarters reveal how families made these stone
walls into homes.

Stop 5 – Living Quarters (Second Level)

Now we enter the living quarters, where families carried out their daily
lives. These small interconnected rooms were carved with simple tools,
yet they became homes for generations. Beds were little more than mats
or animal skins spread across the floor. Furniture was minimal: carved
benches, low tables, and niches in the walls used as shelves.
Picture the scene. An oil lamp flickers on a ledge, casting shadows
across the stone. A mother kneads dough to bake bread on the hearth.
Children play quietly with carved wooden toys. An elder tells stories,
keeping traditions alive even in the darkness. Life underground was
difficult, but it was also filled with resilience.
These quarters remind us that Kaymaklı was not just a fortress but a
community. People did not hide here briefly; they could spend weeks or
months underground, turning bare caves into living spaces filled with
the warmth of family.
From here, the passage continues to the kitchens, where the scent of
smoke and food still lingers on the walls.

Stop 6 – Kitchens & Ovens

This chamber is blackened with soot — a clear sign that it once served
as a kitchen. Look for the carved hearths where fires were lit. Clever
ventilation shafts above carried smoke away, preventing enemies from
spotting life below. This simple yet brilliant system allowed families
to cook without fear of discovery.
Meals here were plain but nourishing. Bread baked on flat stones, stews
made of lentils, beans, or chickpeas simmered slowly. Sometimes dried
meat or cheese added variety. Imagine children huddled around the fire
while their parents prepared food, the smell of fresh bread bringing
comfort even in hard times.
Cooking underground was not without danger — sparks could set straw
alight, and smoke could suffocate if not managed carefully. That is why
these kitchens were designed with such precision. Standing here, you can
almost hear the crackle of fire and the chatter of families gathered for
a meal.
Now, follow the passage forward to discover Kaymaklı’s winemaking
tradition.

Stop 7 – Wine Production Area

Here you find one of the most fascinating parts of Kaymaklı: the wine
press. Look at the shallow basin carved into the stone floor. Grapes
were poured here and crushed by foot, their juice running through
channels into storage jars. Even underground, Cappadocians preserved
their ancient tradition of winemaking.
Wine was more than a drink. It was a source of calories, a trade item,
and, for early Christians, an essential element of the Eucharist.
Imagine the ritual of pressing grapes in this dim chamber, oil lamps
flickering, families working side by side to produce the year’s supply.
Some scholars believe these rooms doubled as communal gathering points,
where stories and songs lightened the burden of labor. Even in hiding,
culture and tradition endured. This winemaking area is proof that
Kaymaklı was not just about survival, but about preserving identity.
From here, let’s continue to one of the most impressive features of the
underground city — the great ventilation shaft.

Stop 8 – Ventilation Shaft

Before you rises one of the marvels of Kaymaklı’s engineering: the great
ventilation shaft. It drops and rises through multiple levels, bringing
fresh air into the heart of the city. Without it, thousands of people,
animals, and fires could not have survived underground.
Look up, and you may catch a glimmer of light from above. This shaft
also doubled as a well, providing water to those below. Its design was
brilliant — wide enough to circulate air, narrow enough to defend. If
attackers tried to pour poison or smoke down, defenders could block it
with stone.
Imagine the relief of cool air flowing through these passages after days
underground. This shaft truly was the city’s lungs. Standing here, you
feel the genius of the builders, who carved not only homes but a fully
functioning ecosystem beneath the earth.
Now, follow the tunnel to the third level, where faith found a home in
hidden chapels.

Stop 9 – Church / Chapel (Third Level)

Here you enter a sacred chamber: the underground church. Its floor plan
is carved in the shape of a cross, with niches that once held simple
altars. On the walls, faint traces of red-ochre crosses can still be
seen. This was a place of worship for families who lived in constant
fear above.
Picture the scene: oil lamps burning low, families gathering silently, a
priest leading prayers that echoed softly through the stone. Even in
times of hiding, faith endured. For early Christians, worshipping
underground was not only practical but symbolic — a reminder that their
faith was unshakable, even when driven beneath the earth.
This chapel shows us that survival was not only physical but also
spiritual. People prayed for safety, for food to last, for children to
grow strong. These walls absorbed centuries of whispered devotion.
Leaving the church, continue toward the large pantries and storage halls
that kept this city alive.

Stop 10 – Pantry & Large Storage Areas

These massive storage chambers reveal the scale of life in Kaymaklı.
Imagine the sight: rows of giant amphorae filled with grain, wine, dried
beans, and nuts. Without these reserves, survival underground would have
been impossible.
Look for carved hollows in the floor where jars once sat securely.
Archaeologists believe supplies were rationed carefully. Leaders
distributed food in measured portions, ensuring fairness and preventing
panic. These rooms demonstrate that Kaymaklı was not chaos but an
organized society.
Now imagine the human side: families gathering here to receive rations,
children peeking curiously into jars, the relief of knowing food was
available while danger loomed above. These storerooms were symbols of
security in an uncertain world.
Next, we move to the defense systems that protected all of this.

Stop 11 – Defense Systems

Here you encounter one of Kaymaklı’s most iconic features: the rolling
stone doors. These massive circular discs could be pushed into place,
sealing corridors completely. Attackers who managed to enter could be
trapped between doors and ambushed from above through hidden openings.
Notice how the passages narrow. This forced intruders to move slowly and
single file, while defenders could strike with spears or arrows. It was
a masterclass in defensive design. To us, these look like simple stone
corridors, but to the people of Kaymaklı they were shields and weapons.
Imagine the tension in these tunnels during an invasion — defenders
holding their breath, waiting in silence, then rolling the door into
place with a thunderous echo. These doors were more than stone; they
were the guardians of a hidden world.
Now, continue to the larger communal halls where decisions were made.

Stop 12 – Meeting Halls (Fourth Level)

This chamber is larger, with space for many people to gather. It served
as a meeting hall where elders and leaders planned strategies for
survival. Here, decisions about food, defense, and faith were made.
Imagine the voices of elders echoing against the walls, children
listening quietly, women contributing advice. This was the underground
parliament of Kaymaklı, proving that life here was structured and
communal.
Some scholars suggest these halls also functioned as schools, where
children were taught to read scripture or learn trades. In times of
crisis, knowledge was as important as food. These halls gave people
purpose, not just shelter.
From here, you will move to the simplest yet most emotional areas — the
bare refuges where families waited in silence.

Stop 13 – Refuge & Shelter Areas

These small, empty rooms may look plain, but they were vital. When
attacks above lasted for days, entire families crowded into these
shelters. Oil lamps burned low to save fuel, children slept in their
mothers’ arms, and neighbors shared stories to calm each other.
Silence was survival. Any noise risked detection. Imagine dozens of
people breathing quietly, whispering prayers, their hearts beating fast
while they waited for the danger to pass. These chambers carry the
weight of fear but also of solidarity.
They remind us that Kaymaklı was not just stone walls, but human lives
bound together by resilience. As you leave, remember the courage of
those who endured here.
Now, let’s follow the passage upward to return to daylight.

Stop 14 – Exit Passage

As you ascend the sloping passage, notice how the air changes. The cool,
heavy underground atmosphere gives way to fresh light. Emerging from
Kaymaklı is a powerful moment — after experiencing the hidden world
below, daylight feels different.
This was the feeling for those who once lived here. After days or weeks
underground, stepping into the sun was both relief and renewal. The
underground city was a fortress of survival, but life above was always
the hope.
Kaymaklı stands today as a testament to human ingenuity and
perseverance. Carved by hand, lived in by generations, it still whispers
stories of fear, faith, and survival. As you leave, take a final glance
back and remember: beneath this quiet village lies one of the greatest
hidden cities of the ancient world.
