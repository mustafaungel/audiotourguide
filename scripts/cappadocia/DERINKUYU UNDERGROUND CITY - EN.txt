Introduction – General Orientation

Welcome to Derinkuyu Underground City, the deepest and one of the most
astonishing subterranean settlements in the world. Beneath your feet
lies a city carved entirely into soft volcanic rock, extending down
nearly 85 meters with multiple levels. Archaeologists have explored at
least 18 floors, though only 8 are currently open to visitors. At its
peak, Derinkuyu is believed to have sheltered between 20,000 and 30,000
people, along with their livestock and supplies.
The earliest parts of this underground labyrinth may date back to the
Phrygian period around the 8th century BCE, later expanded by early
Christians fleeing persecution and perfected during Byzantine times as a
place of refuge from Arab raids. In later centuries, it continued to
serve as a safe haven against Mongols and even into the Ottoman era.
Derinkuyu is not just a collection of tunnels. It contains stables,
kitchens, wineries, living quarters, schools, churches, ventilation
shafts, wells, and defense systems. It is essentially a complete city
underground, designed to sustain thousands of people for months at a
time.
As you prepare to enter, keep in mind that this is not simply an
archaeological site. You are about to walk the very same passages where
families once lived in secrecy, in fear, but also with remarkable
resilience and ingenuity.

  

Stop 1 – Entrance & First Level (Stables)

*“As you step through the narrow entrance passage, daylight fades
quickly and the air begins to feel cooler. You are now standing on the
first level of Derinkuyu Underground City, the gateway into a world that
stretches nearly 85 meters beneath your feet. This wide chamber around
you was not a living room or a hall for gatherings — it was a stable,
the very first priority for survival underground.

Look closely at the long troughs carved into the stone along the walls.
These feeding basins once held hay and water for horses, goats, and
sheep. When danger threatened the villages above, animals were driven
quickly into this level, their hooves echoing against the rock as
families guided them down the sloping passages. Imagine the noise: the
bleating of goats, the neighing of horses, the shouts of children trying
to keep the animals calm, all mixing together in the dim light of oil
lamps.

Keeping livestock underground may sound strange to us, but to the people
of Derinkuyu it was essential. Animals provided milk, cheese, wool,
meat, and even warmth. In the cold Anatolian winters, the body heat of
dozens of animals helped raise the underground temperature.
Archaeologists believe this level was chosen for stables so that waste
could be removed easily and fresh supplies of hay could be carried in
from the surface. The design was practical, efficient, and necessary.

Take a moment to picture the daily rhythm here. At dawn, families would
feed their animals before preparing food for themselves. Children
learned early how important livestock were; helping with chores was not
optional but vital for survival. During invasions, these stables became
crowded, noisy lifelines — every creature meant one more chance for the
community to endure.

Notice also the size of this hall compared to the passages you walked
through to reach it. Wide spaces allowed animals to gather safely, while
defenders could block the entry behind them with huge rolling stone
doors. This first level shows the genius of Derinkuyu’s design: it was
both a farm and a fortress, a place where life could continue in the
darkest of times.

When you are ready, follow the passage downward. Ahead lies the second
level, where the kitchens and storage rooms reveal how food was prepared
and preserved for the entire underground community.”*

Stop 2 – Second Level (Kitchens & Storage)

*“Descending deeper into Derinkuyu, you now arrive at the second level,
where the heart of daily survival beats strongest — the kitchens and
food storage chambers. Look up at the ceilings and you may notice
patches of black soot, stains left by countless fires that once burned
here. These marks are the fingerprints of meals cooked centuries ago.
Families prepared bread, stews, and dried grains right here, in the
safety of the underground.

The kitchens were cleverly designed. Fire pits were carved into the
floor, and ventilation shafts carried smoke away so that no trace of
life could be seen from above. Without this system, an enemy could
easily detect the underground community by the faintest whiff of smoke.
Imagine the relief of families who could cook a warm meal without fear
of discovery.

Around you are also the storage rooms. Along the walls, niches were cut
into the stone to hold large clay jars, or amphorae, filled with wheat,
beans, dried fruit, olive oil, and even wine. These jars were sealed
with cloth and wax to preserve their contents. The underground’s
constant cool temperature made it a natural refrigerator. Food here
could last for weeks or months, which meant families could hide below
for as long as danger remained above.

Picture the daily rhythm in this chamber. Women kneading dough into
flatbreads while children run back and forth carrying jars of lentils.
Men checking the supplies, carefully rationing so that food would not
run out too quickly. The smell of baking bread mixed with the earthy
scent of stone walls. Every meal was simple, but every bite carried
hope.

These chambers tell us that Derinkuyu was more than a fortress — it was
a community with discipline, order, and planning. Survival underground
required cooperation. Families shared responsibilities, food was
distributed fairly, and neighbors looked after each other. In these
kitchens and storerooms, the underground city was sustained day after
day.

When you are ready, continue your descent. Ahead lies the third level,
where you will see the living quarters — the carved stone homes where
families made a life in the depths of the earth.”*

Stop 3 – Third Level (Living Quarters)

*“You are now stepping into the living quarters of Derinkuyu Underground
City, the heart of family life beneath the earth. These chambers may
seem small and bare today, but centuries ago they were filled with the
warmth, sounds, and routines of everyday life. Look around — the walls
are cut with niches that once held oil lamps, jars, and small
belongings. The floors, once covered with mats and animal skins, served
as sleeping areas.

Picture the scene: an oil lamp flickers, casting long shadows on the
stone. A mother kneads dough while children play quietly nearby with
carved wooden toys. The father may be repairing tools, while
grandparents sit together, telling stories of past generations. Even
underground, life carried on — simple, disciplined, but human in every
sense.

Now imagine the tension during times of danger. Families huddled here in
silence, children soothed to sleep with whispers. The faint crackle of
the fire had to be carefully managed to avoid drawing attention from
above. These living quarters were not just homes; they were sanctuaries
where fear and resilience coexisted.

What strikes archaeologists is the sense of organization. Rooms were
often linked, with small corridors connecting family spaces. There were
areas for sleeping, for cooking, and for gathering. It was a true
neighborhood carved into stone. Despite the cramped conditions, people
created community, routine, and even joy here.

Standing in these chambers, you can almost hear the echoes of laughter,
prayers, and lullabies that once filled the air. These walls remind us
that Derinkuyu was never just about survival. It was a city alive with
human spirit, hidden deep beneath the ground.

When you are ready, follow the passage deeper. Ahead lies the missionary
school and church, where the underground community preserved its faith
and teachings in the shadows of stone.”*

Stop 4 – Fourth Level (Missionary School & Church)

*“Now you stand on the fourth level of Derinkuyu, where survival was not
only about food and shelter but also about faith and knowledge. This
level is home to the missionary school and the church — two spaces that
preserved both education and spirituality in times of darkness.

Look first at the large hall cut into the rock. Along its sides, you can
still see the stone benches where students once sat. This was a
classroom, a place of teaching and learning carved deep beneath the
earth. Imagine the scene: children seated quietly, listening to lessons
about scripture and language, while their teachers used simple tools and
words to pass down essential knowledge. In an era when the world above
was unsafe, this underground school ensured that culture and belief did
not vanish.

Nearby stands the underground church, the spiritual heart of Derinkuyu.
Its design is striking: a barrel-vaulted ceiling and a cruciform
(cross-shaped) layout, echoing the forms of churches above ground.
Though most frescoes are gone, faint traces of red-ochre crosses still
remain on the walls — silent symbols of devotion. Picture the faithful
gathered here, oil lamps glowing dimly, voices rising softly in hymns.
Their prayers echoed through these stone walls, filling the underground
city with a sense of hope.

For the early Christians who once hid here, this church was more than a
place of worship. It was a reminder that even when forced beneath the
earth, their beliefs remained unshaken. The act of teaching, learning,
and worshipping underground was itself a declaration of survival — a
statement that life in Derinkuyu was not merely about enduring but about
preserving identity.

As you stand here, close your eyes for a moment. Can you hear the echoes
of whispered prayers, the soft murmur of hymns, the steady rhythm of a
teacher’s voice guiding his students? These sounds once filled this
level, blending faith with resilience.

When you are ready, continue deeper. Ahead lies the fifth level, where
the great ventilation shafts reveal the genius of the city’s
engineering.”*

Stop 5 – Fifth Level (Ventilation Shafts)

*“Welcome to one of the true marvels of Derinkuyu — the great
ventilation shafts. Standing here, you are looking at the very lungs of
the underground city. Without these shafts, life below ground would not
have lasted even a few hours, let alone weeks or months.

The main shaft stretches more than 55 meters deep, connecting nearly
every level of the city. Imagine thousands of people, hundreds of
animals, dozens of kitchens and fires — all producing heat, smoke, and
carbon dioxide. Without fresh air, survival would have been impossible.
But the builders of Derinkuyu solved this problem with genius. They
carved this massive column of air to circulate naturally, bringing cool,
clean oxygen into every chamber. Even today, if you stand quietly, you
may feel a faint draft moving through the tunnels.

Look up and you might glimpse a sliver of light from the surface far
above. This shaft was not only for air but also for water. Ingeniously,
it doubled as a well, ensuring fresh, untainted water was available on
every level. The well was protected carefully so that enemies on the
surface could not poison it — another example of the defensive
brilliance of this underground world.

Picture families gathering around here to fill their jars. Children
waiting in line, mothers carrying heavy amphorae back to their kitchens,
elders thanking God for the simple gift of water. This was a lifeline
that connected all who lived below. In times of siege, when the world
above was hostile, this shaft meant life could go on uninterrupted
underground.

It is easy to take air and water for granted, but for the people of
Derinkuyu, these shafts were miracles carved by human hands. They remind
us that this was not just a city of stone but a city of survival —
carefully planned, ingeniously executed, and sustained by necessity.

From here, your journey continues deeper still. The sixth level awaits,
where wine presses and vast storerooms reveal how food and culture were
preserved in the depths of the earth.”*

Stop 6 – Sixth Level (Wine Presses & Large Storage)

*“You are now standing on the sixth level of Derinkuyu, a place where
practicality met tradition. Here, the community carved out wine presses
and vast storage chambers — proof that life underground was not only
about survival, but also about preserving culture and identity.

Look at the floor carefully. You’ll notice shallow circular basins cut
into the stone, connected to small channels. These were wine presses.
Grapes harvested from the vineyards above ground were carried down in
baskets, poured into these basins, and then crushed by foot. The juice
flowed through the channels into large jars, where it was left to
ferment. Imagine the scene: men stomping rhythmically in unison,
children laughing at their purple-stained feet, women sealing jars with
cloth and wax. Even in times of danger, the tradition of winemaking
continued.

But wine was more than a drink. For the early Christians who lived here,
it was essential for religious rituals — especially the Eucharist.
Producing it underground was both practical and symbolic, a way of
keeping faith alive in the shadows.

Surrounding you are the storage chambers. Large amphorae once filled
these spaces, packed tightly into niches carved into the walls. These
jars held dried beans, wheat, lentils, nuts, and fruits — the staples of
survival. Food was carefully rationed, and responsibility was shared
across families. Imagine the relief of knowing that while enemies
marched above, food supplies hidden below ensured another day of life.

Think about the organization required to manage these stores. Leaders
monitored supplies, guards protected the chambers, and families waited
their turn to receive rations. It was discipline that kept the
underground city alive. Without these storerooms, Derinkuyu could not
have sheltered thousands for so long.

As you take in this level, reflect on what it represents: not only
nourishment for the body, but continuity of faith, tradition, and
resilience. When you are ready, descend further to the seventh level,
where wells and deep shelters offered the greatest protection in times
of crisis.”*

Stop 7 – Seventh Level (Wells & Deep Shelters)

*“Now you’ve reached the seventh level of Derinkuyu, where the city’s
deepest secrets come into view. Here, survival depended on two essential
elements: fresh water and safe shelter. Without these, no community — no
matter how well organized — could endure.

The wells you see here are extraordinary. They descend even deeper than
this level, connecting directly to underground aquifers. Unlike ordinary
wells, they were designed with security in mind. Openings on the surface
could be sealed off, preventing enemies from poisoning or contaminating
the water supply. Imagine how critical this was: in times of siege, when
entire families and animals hid below for weeks, these wells guaranteed
clean water. Every drop carried hope.

Picture the scene: children lining up with clay jars, their parents
filling them carefully, elders giving thanks for this gift. In the
silence of the underground, the soft sound of dripping water would have
been a comfort — a reminder that life could continue.

Alongside the wells are the deep shelters. These large halls and narrow
chambers were where families gathered during the most dangerous times.
When invaders stormed the land above, people retreated here,
extinguishing most lamps to conserve oil, sitting quietly in the
darkness. Imagine dozens of families packed closely together, children
lulled to sleep in their mothers’ arms, neighbors whispering prayers to
keep fear at bay. Silence was survival.

Think of the psychological strength this demanded. Days, sometimes weeks
underground, without sunlight, without certainty. Yet people endured
because they trusted the stone around them, the wells beneath them, and
the unity of their community. These shelters remind us that survival was
not only physical but also emotional — a triumph of solidarity over
despair.

Take a moment to breathe in the cool air. Ahead of you lies the deepest
accessible level of Derinkuyu, where communal halls held entire groups
together through the darkest of times.”*

Stop 8 – Eighth Level (Communal Halls & Shelters)

*“You have now reached the deepest level of Derinkuyu that is open to
visitors — the eighth floor. Standing here, you are more than 55 meters
beneath the surface. The air feels cooler, the ceilings are lower, and
the silence is heavier. These are the communal halls and shelters, the
last refuge for families when the world above was at its most dangerous.

Look at the size of these chambers. They were designed to hold large
groups of people together, sometimes hundreds at a time. Families slept
side by side, mats spread across the stone floor, neighbors sharing what
little space they had. The sense of community here was powerful. When
fear threatened to overwhelm, unity provided strength.

Imagine the atmosphere during a siege. Oil lamps burned dimly to save
precious fuel, their light casting faint orange glows on the walls. The
air was filled with hushed voices — parents calming children, elders
leading whispered prayers, the occasional sob muffled in the dark. In
the distance, the slow drip of water echoed through the silence. These
sounds became the rhythm of life underground.

Life here was far from easy. There was little privacy, little comfort.
Food and water were rationed carefully, and the air often grew thick
despite the shafts above. But in these communal shelters, people endured
together. They shared stories, sang hymns softly, and reminded each
other that the danger above would eventually pass.

Think about the psychological resilience required. To spend days or even
weeks here demanded not just physical endurance but immense faith. Faith
in God, faith in neighbors, and faith in the stone walls carved by their
ancestors. This was survival at its rawest, but also at its most
inspiring.

As you stand in the deepest accessible chambers of Derinkuyu, let the
weight of history surround you. Here, thousands of lives once pulsed
with fear and courage. When you are ready, begin your ascent upward.
Along the way, you’ll discover the defense systems that made this
endurance possible.”*

Stop 9 – Defense Systems (All Levels)

*“As you make your way back upward through Derinkuyu, it becomes clear
that this underground city was not only a refuge but also a fortress.
Defense was at the heart of its design. Every passage, every chamber,
every door had a dual purpose: to serve the community and to protect it
from those who threatened their survival.

Look closely at the notches in the walls near some of the corridors.
These marks were carved to hold the great circular stone doors — immense
discs that could weigh up to half a ton. In times of danger, defenders
rolled these stones into place with wooden levers, sealing off entire
sections of the city. Once closed, there was no way an intruder could
move them from the outside. To the people below, these doors meant
safety; to enemies, they were dead ends.

The narrow design of the tunnels also worked in the defenders’ favor.
Invaders were forced to crawl or stoop single file, making them easy
targets. Small openings above some corridors allowed defenders to attack
from hidden vantage points — thrusting spears, pouring sand, or even hot
oil. The underground city was a trap as much as a shelter, turning the
landscape into a battlefield of stone.

Imagine the silence before an ambush. Families waiting in darkness,
children held close, while defenders pressed against the walls,
listening for the sound of footsteps. Then, the sudden thunder of a
stone door rolling into place, sealing the invaders’ fate. These were
moments of tension, but also of triumph, as the underground city proved
unbreakable.

What makes Derinkuyu extraordinary is how seamlessly defense was woven
into daily life. A corridor that carried children to school could also
become a choke point for enemies. A kitchen doorway could double as a
fortified barrier. Security was not an afterthought; it was built into
the very bones of the city.

As you continue toward the exit, keep this in mind: Derinkuyu was not
just a place to hide. It was a stronghold designed with intelligence,
skill, and determination — a masterpiece of survival architecture that
turned the earth itself into protection.”*

Stop 10 – Exit & Reflection

*“As you ascend the final passages of Derinkuyu, the air begins to
change. The heaviness of the underground lifts, and the faint glow of
daylight filters through the stone corridors. Step by step, you return
to the surface — just as families once did after days or even weeks of
hiding. Imagine the feeling of that first breath of fresh air, the
relief of seeing the sky again, the joy of knowing the danger had
passed. For the people of Derinkuyu, emerging into the sunlight was not
only survival, it was rebirth.

Pause for a moment at the exit and look back in your mind’s eye. Beneath
your feet lies a world carved by human hands: stables where animals gave
warmth and sustenance, kitchens where bread baked in secrecy, classrooms
where children whispered lessons, churches where faith echoed in the
dark, wells that brought life, and defenses that held strong against
invaders. Together, they formed a city — a complete, functioning society
hidden beneath the earth.

Derinkuyu is more than stone and tunnels; it is a story of resilience.
Generations of people trusted this underground world to keep them alive.
They endured silence, hunger, and fear, but they also shared laughter,
stories, and hope. These walls remember both their hardships and their
courage.

Today, as you walk back into the open air, you are not just leaving an
archaeological site. You are retracing the footsteps of thousands who
lived, prayed, worked, and survived here. Their determination still
whispers through every passage.

Derinkuyu stands as one of humanity’s greatest monuments to ingenuity
and faith. It is proof that when faced with unimaginable hardship,
people can adapt, create, and endure. As you leave, carry with you not
just the memory of stone walls and dark tunnels, but the spirit of those
who transformed fear into survival.

Thank you for exploring Derinkuyu Underground City, and for taking the
time to step into the hidden world that lies beneath Cappadocia.”*
